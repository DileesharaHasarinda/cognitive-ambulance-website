Introduction

Emergency medical services in Sri Lanka face serious delays caused by traffic congestion, non-compliant civilian drivers, ambulance crew fatigue, and incomplete patient information. Previous studies on emergency vehicle prioritization, ambulance tracking, and smart traffic clearance show that traffic congestion directly affects emergency response efficiency [1], [4], [5]. Occupational fatigue among emergency responders has also been identified as a major factor that can increase injury risk and reduce responder performance [2].

Incomplete patient information is another major challenge in emergency care. Personal health record systems have been studied as a method to improve health information exchange and patient safety [3], while emergency department studies show that digital health record access can support clinician-assessed patient care [8]. These issues reduce response speed, affect patient outcomes, and increase safety risks for both responders and patients.

In addition to human emergency care, veterinary emergency response also suffers from fragmented, subjective information. Pet owners often describe symptoms under stress, while veterinarians lack access to real-time physiological data before the animal arrives. Recent smart animal health monitoring and AI-IoT pet care studies show the value of real-time physiological tracking in animal care [10], [11], [12].

This research proposes an extended Smart Ambulance Ecosystem that integrates four complementary edge-optimized components into one unified platform: crew monitoring, smartphone-based traffic clearance, patient risk prediction, and AI-powered pre-veterinary emergency triage. Related studies on computer vision-based drowsiness detection and transformer-based driver drowsiness detection demonstrate the potential of AI for safety-critical monitoring tasks [6], [7].

The system is designed for low-resource settings, uses Raspberry Pi 5 with Flutter and React interfaces, requires no roadside hardware, supports offline fallback, and enables two coordinated phases: transit and arrival handover. Clinical text summarization research further supports the use of AI-assisted information processing for improving medical decision support and handover efficiency [9].

Literature Review

Previous work in emergency response has examined crew fatigue detection, traffic prioritization, portable health records, and clinical summarization as separate research areas. Studies show that occupational fatigue significantly increases injury risk among responders [2], while traffic-based emergency vehicle prioritization approaches mainly focus on ambulance tracking, smart traffic clearance, and signal control mechanisms [1], [4], [5]. Existing patient-controlled health record systems support health information exchange and patient safety [3], and recent emergency department studies show the potential value of digital health records in clinical decision-making [8]. However, these systems still lack integrated edge-AI risk prediction, fast QR-based emergency access, and a unified emergency workflow. Clinical summarization research using large language models further shows the potential of automated medical text processing, but it is generally studied separately from emergency response platforms [9].

In veterinary emergency care, recent research on pet wearables and smart health monitoring has shown the value of real-time physiological tracking [10], [11], [12]. However, most existing systems do not combine wearable data, owner-reported symptom input, urgency triage, and pre-arrival veterinary alert packages into one unified emergency workflow. Driver drowsiness and fatigue detection studies also demonstrate the usefulness of AI-based monitoring in safety-critical environments [6], [7], but these approaches are not directly integrated with emergency patient records, responder dispatch, or veterinary response workflows. The current literature therefore remains fragmented, with no fully integrated platform covering all critical response stages for both human and companion animal emergencies.

Key Insights

Low-Resource Relevance

Emergency systems in Sri Lanka need low-cost, hardware-light, and offline-capable solutions.

Parallel Intelligence

Fatigue monitoring, road clearance, patient risk prediction, and veterinary triage can run together.

Edge-First Advantage

Local inference reduces latency and improves resilience under unstable connectivity conditions.

Unified Handover

AI-generated summaries and risk insights support faster, better-informed treatment decisions.

Core Components

01

Crew Fatigue & Stress Monitoring

Uses MediaPipe Face Landmarker, a quantized MobileNetV2 CNN, and LSTM to monitor ambulance driver and paramedic fatigue and stress through PERCLOS, EAR, head pose, and mouth-pattern indicators.

02

Smartphone-Based Traffic Clearance

Uses GPS and accelerometer data from nearby drivers’ smartphones with a Random Forest classifier to predict yielding behavior and trigger tiered alerts with personalized alarms.

03

Patient Risk Prediction & QR Access

Gives patients a consent-based lock-screen QR widget for emergency access to digital health profiles, while an edge AI model classifies risk and produces contextual summaries.

04

Pre-Veterinary Emergency Triage

Integrates wearable pet data, owner-reported symptoms, and AI triage models to produce urgency classification and a Pre-Vet Alert Package for real-time veterinary preparation.

Research Gaps

Fragmented EMS Solutions

Existing systems treat fatigue monitoring, traffic clearance, and patient information as separate functions.

Roadside Hardware Dependence

Many traffic-prioritization systems rely on infrastructure that is too expensive for wide rural deployment.

Limited Edge-AI Handover Support

Few systems provide AI-generated summaries and risk prediction directly on edge devices for hospital handover.

Veterinary Emergency Gap

No existing system fully combines wearable pet data, owner symptom reports, urgency triage, and pre-arrival veterinary alerts in one platform.

Research Questions

RQ1: How can edge AI improve real-time crew fatigue and stress monitoring in ambulance environments?
RQ2: How can smartphone-only sensing be used to improve driver compliance and ambulance road clearance?
RQ3: How can QR-enabled digital health records and edge-AI risk prediction improve emergency handover?
RQ4: How can wearable pet data and owner-reported symptoms be fused for real-time veterinary emergency triage?
RQ5: How can all four components operate together in a unified low-power platform with offline fallback?

Research Objectives

Objective 1

Develop a dual-role fatigue and stress monitoring system for ambulance drivers and paramedics.

Objective 2

Design a smartphone-based traffic clearance module that predicts yielding behavior without roadside hardware.

Objective 3

Implement QR-enabled patient health access and edge-based medical risk classification for emergency handover.

Objective 4

Build an AI-powered pre-veterinary triage module that classifies urgency using wearable and symptom data.
